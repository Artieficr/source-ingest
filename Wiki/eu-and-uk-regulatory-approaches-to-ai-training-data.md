# EU and UK Regulatory Approaches to AI Training Data

## Content
The European Union and United Kingdom are approaching AI training data through transparency and licensing frameworks rather than a US-style fair-use doctrine, since the EU has no direct equivalent to fair use.

The EU finalized its AI Act in late 2023, but its copyright-related provisions focus mainly on disclosure of outputs rather than regulating training data directly. Separately, GDPR creates a privacy angle: using personal data for model training can raise consent issues, though companies often rely on claims of "legitimate interest" or anonymization to justify their practices. In 2024, the EU Digital Services Act began requiring AI systems to keep logs of training and output data for potential regulatory audits, though these logs are not required to be public and enforcement details remain pending. The European Data Protection Board issued non-binding guidance in December 2023 suggesting privacy safeguards for AI training data, but did not impose hard rules, while GDPR enforcement against AI training on personal data without consent continues separately.

The UK government launched a consultation on AI and copyright in October 2023. Under current UK law, copying works for training requires a license, with no broad exception for AI. Creators have complained they lack control or compensation, while AI companies argue legal uncertainty stifles innovation. A UK proposal aims to require AI developers to be transparent about what content they use for training and to introduce a new exception for non-commercial text and data mining -- but only if rights holders are able to opt out, similar to a "robots.txt for AI" concept. The overall UK goal is for AI models to be trained under clear licenses or exceptions and for AI outputs to be appropriately labeled.

Meta's 2025 announcement that it would train on EU users' public posts, with a required opt-out mechanism, was widely understood as a response to these EU legal pressures rather than a voluntary policy choice. Overall, no international consensus exists: US agencies and recent court rulings lean toward permitting AI training under certain conditions, while the EU and UK are still developing new transparency and licensing rules.
