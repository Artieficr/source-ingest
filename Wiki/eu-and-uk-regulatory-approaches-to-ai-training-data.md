# EU and UK Regulatory Approaches to AI Training Data

## Content
Unlike the U.S., the EU has no direct fair-use doctrine, but several existing and emerging frameworks affect AI training there. The EU finalized its AI Act in late 2023, though its copyright-related provisions focus mainly on requiring disclosure about outputs rather than regulating training data directly. Separately, GDPR creates a privacy angle: using personal data for model training can raise consent issues, though companies often rely on "legitimate interest" justifications or claim the data has been anonymized. In 2024, the EU Digital Services Act began requiring AI systems to keep logs of training and output data for potential regulatory audits, though these logs need not be made public and enforcement details remain pending. The European Data Protection Board issued non-binding guidance in December 2023 recommending privacy safeguards for AI training data, without imposing hard rules, while also continuing to enforce GDPR against unauthorized use of personal data in AI.

The UK has taken a more consultative approach. In October 2023 it launched a consultation on AI and copyright, noting that under current UK law, copying works for training requires a license — there is no broad exception. Creators argue they lack control or compensation, while AI firms argue legal uncertainty stifles innovation. The UK's proposal (expected 2024) aims to require AI developers to be transparent about what content they use for training, and to introduce a new text-and-data-mining exception for non-commercial use — but only if rights holders are given the ability to opt out. The broader goal is for AI models to be trained either under license or under a clear opt-out-respecting exception, with appropriate labeling of AI outputs.

Meta's 2025 announcement that EU users could opt out of having their public posts used for AI training was widely understood as a response to these EU legal pressures, and was noted as consistent with steps other companies had also taken in the region. Overall, no international consensus exists: U.S. agencies (Copyright Office) lean toward rights holders even as some U.S. courts have sided with developers (Anthropic, Meta), while the EU and UK are still in the process of considering new transparency and licensing rules.
