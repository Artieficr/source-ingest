# Dr. Matt Jones's Personal Burnout During Medical Training

## Content
During a stretch of his medical school and residency training, Dr. Matt Jones described going through a period where he tried to optimize everything in his life simultaneously, while surrounded by peers in the medical field he describes as "superhumans" — people operating at levels of productivity he felt he needed to match. His routine at the time included sleep tracking, nutrition logging, reading goals that he tracked and spoke about out loud, two separate workout protocols, meditation, journaling, and an actively managed social media presence — all optimized in detail, but each pursued at, by his own estimate, only about 40% effectiveness.

This schedule left him with almost no real social life; even when physically present with other people, he was mentally occupied thinking about his next social media post, how to optimize his current situation, or whether the time was being "wasted." He also described continuously adding new habits by trying to implement ideas pulled from books he was reading every day, which made his list of commitments grow longer and longer over time. This pattern escalated until he passed out from exhaustion one day in clinic — an event he describes as a major wake-up call.

Following that incident, he cut back to two or three things that actually moved the needle for him personally: training consistently, protecting his sleep, and not touching his phone in the morning. After making this change, his output went up drastically, because he stopped spending energy managing an overly complex set of systems that were too much to actually maintain. He frames this experience as the basis for the broader argument that the goal isn't to build the most impressive-looking routine — unless you're specifically trying to look impressive on social media — but to find and protect the few things that genuinely move your life forward.
