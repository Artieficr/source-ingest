# Policy and Practical Recommendations for AI Training Data Transparency

## Content
Given the limits of current technology, several regulatory and practical measures have been proposed to address the AI training verification gap, alongside concrete advice for different groups of stakeholders.

Proposed regulatory options include mandatory disclosure requirements, where AI companies would be required to publicly state what categories of data they use for training (e.g., percentage from public web, licensed sources, or user submissions) and update this information periodically -- similar to nutrition labels, an idea reflected in the UK's consultation on public registers of training datasets or hash commitments. Opt-out and licensing regimes are also proposed, such as the UK's idea of a text-and-data-mining exception with opt-out by default, or an EU-style exception requiring respect for "robots.txt"-like AI-reservation tags on web content. Audit mandates would require independent audits of training data policies, particularly for high-risk AI systems, building on the EU AI Act's framework for third-party conformity assessments, though that framework does not yet specifically address training data. Contractual remedies could involve platforms adding terms-of-service clauses prohibiting their content from being used to train LLMs without consent, enforceable via breach-of-contract claims, though this is more practical for premium licensed data sources than for general web scraping. Copyright law reform proposals include compulsory licensing schemes -- discussed by the US Copyright Office -- under which AI developers would pay a statutory fee to use copyrighted books, similar to music licensing organizations like ASCAP. Finally, stronger enforcement and penalties from bodies like the FTC or EU data protection authorities could penalize companies that misrepresent their data practices, treating false "we don't train on your data" claims as unfair or deceptive, though such enforcement has so far been rare.

For content creators, the practical recommendation is to recognize that control over AI use of their work is currently limited, to state clearly in licenses or platform terms whether their content may be used for AI training, and to consider technological markers such as watermarks or distinctive "seed" sentences that could later be searched for in model outputs. For end users concerned about privacy, the advice is to prefer paid or enterprise tiers, confirm opt-out settings are enabled, avoid relying on confidentiality in free-tier chats, and treat any submitted data as potentially influencing future models. For policymakers, recommended steps include implementing transparency requirements such as dataset registers or third-party audits, encouraging opt-out standards akin to robots.txt, supporting research into watermarking and tracing, and clarifying fair use or licensing frameworks. For regulators and litigators, existing tools -- unfair competition law, data protection law, and contract law -- could be applied while monitoring how models reproduce copyrighted text and how terms of service are enforced. The overarching conclusion is that no simple technical test currently exists to verify training-data claims, so progress will likely require a combination of policy mandates, technical innovation, and market pressure for accountability.
