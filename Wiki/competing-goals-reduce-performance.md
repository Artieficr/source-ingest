# Competing Goals Reduce Performance

## Content
When multiple goals compete for the same limited resources — time, attention, and mental energy — performance on all of them drops, not just on the lower-priority ones. A specific pattern shows up in research: when people are given both a quantity goal and a quality goal at the same time, they more often sacrifice quality in favor of quantity. People also tend to gravitate toward whatever task feels completable rather than whatever actually matters, hunting for easy wins while the genuinely important work gets pushed back and deferred indefinitely.

This dynamic isn't limited to individuals — research across organizations shows that companies with fewer strategic priorities significantly outperform those juggling many priorities at once. The same pattern plays out for individuals on a daily basis: when someone tries to run ten things simultaneously, they tend to make some progress on everything but actually finish nothing — or at least fail to finish the things that matter most. The broader lesson is that not everything on a list needs to be treated as a priority, and the people who execute well over long periods of time consistently have fewer active commitments running at once, not more, while still getting significantly more done than people juggling a much larger plate.
