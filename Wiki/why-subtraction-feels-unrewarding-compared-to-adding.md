# Why Subtraction Feels Unrewarding Compared to Adding

## Content
When people feel stuck or behind, their instinctive first move is almost always to add something -- a new habit, system, goal, or piece of structure they read about or saw in a video -- because adding feels like effort and feels like taking the problem seriously. Subtracting something, by contrast, gives no equivalent feedback: there is no moment of satisfaction when a goal is removed from a list.

Social media reinforces this bias, since it's the dominant medium through which people consume self-improvement content and it rewards visible effort and a growing list of activities -- examples include "perfect morning routine" content or claims like reading multiple books a day. However, many people broadcasting these large lists of activities actually maintain quite small to-do lists themselves; the heavily publicized activity is often their one standout thing, performed for the appearance of productivity. Doing less doesn't look ambitious in this framing, even though it is often the more ambitious and effective move, because it tends to produce far better results than a long, scattered list of commitments.
