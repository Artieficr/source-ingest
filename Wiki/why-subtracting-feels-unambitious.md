# Why Subtracting Feels Unambitious

## Content
Adding something new to your routine feels like effort — it feels like you're taking a problem seriously and being more serious about moving forward. Subtracting something, by contrast, gives no such feedback: there's no moment of satisfaction when you remove a goal from your list, even though doing so is often the more impactful move.

This bias is reinforced by social media culture, which rewards highly visible effort — the perfect morning routine, reading a book a day (or, in one example cited, someone claiming to read seven books a day, which isn't realistic). The culture rewards a growing, visible list of things someone is "working on," even though many of the people projecting this image actually maintain a fairly small to-do list themselves — they're being rewarded for the appearance of effort by publicizing one thing as if it represents their whole approach. Doing less doesn't look ambitious from the outside, even though it's often the far more ambitious move, because a smaller, more focused set of commitments is what actually gets people further over time. People who genuinely execute well over long stretches — not just talk or post about it — are recognizable by protecting a small number of things with significant intensity, even though this looks unimpressive compared to someone broadcasting a long list of habits and routines.
