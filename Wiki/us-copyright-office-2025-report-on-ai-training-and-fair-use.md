# US Copyright Office 2025 Report on AI Training and Fair Use

## Content
On May 9, 2025, the U.S. Copyright Office issued a 108-page report addressing AI training and fair use, taking a notably cautious stance toward AI developers. The report concluded that using copyrighted works for AI training is not automatically fair use, and warned that if a model's outputs closely mirror its training inputs, even the model's weights themselves could be considered infringing.

The Office explicitly rejected simplistic analogies comparing AI training to human learning (e.g., "AI reading books is like a person reading books"), and cautioned that AI training processes can effectively create "perfect copies" of expressive works internally. It also urged courts and policymakers to weigh market dilution — the idea that if a trained model can compete with the style or genre of original works, that competitive harm should count against a fair use finding, separate from any direct verbatim copying.

Overall, the report leaned toward protecting rights holders, concluding that some common industry practices — particularly mass ingestion of unlicensed or pirated content for commercial purposes — likely "go beyond established fair use boundaries." The Office stopped short of recommending new legislation, but signaled that existing law might not adequately protect creators if AI training practices continue unchecked. While not binding law itself, the report has influenced subsequent policy debate and is referenced by plaintiffs in ongoing litigation. It sits alongside a cluster of major 2025 legal developments — including the Bartz v. Anthropic and Kadrey v. Meta rulings issued the following month — that together mark a period of rapid legal activity around AI training data.
