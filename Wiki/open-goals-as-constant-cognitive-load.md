# Open Goals as Constant Cognitive Load

## Content
Every unfinished commitment, untouched goal, or lingering to-do list item functions like an open background app on your brain — it doesn't sit inertly waiting to be reopened, but keeps running and consuming cognitive resources continuously, whether or not you're consciously aware of it. Research on this shows that unresolved decisions actually drain more cognitive energy than unfinished tasks do. This means a goal you haven't really started, or a habit you keep telling yourself you'll get to, costs more mental bandwidth than if you had simply decided not to pursue it at all — deciding not to do something effectively closes that background task and stops the drain.

When someone is technically "working on" 15 things, their brain isn't only carrying that load while they sit down to focus — it's carrying all 15 all the time, as a constant cognitive tax. The result is a persistent, low-grade exhaustion: a feeling of being worn out before even starting a task, or burning out within minutes of beginning one. This feeling is often misattributed to laziness, but the more accurate explanation is simply having too much running in the background simultaneously, for too long, such that progress feels stalled even though enormous mental energy is being spent just maintaining the open loops.
