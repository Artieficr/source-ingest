# The Subtraction Exercise

## Content
This is a practical self-audit for identifying which commitments are actually worth keeping. First, write down everything you're currently doing — every habit, goal, system, and commitment you've made to yourself — without filtering anything out, whether on paper or in a notes app. Second, go through the list and ask: in the last 30 days, which of these have I actually done consistently? Mark those items (circle them or add a star). Third, from that narrowed set, ask a further question: of the things I've actually been doing, which ones have produced something real — better energy, better output, more happiness, better health, or anything else you can point to as a tangible result? Mark those as well.

Whatever remains after this second filter is what deserves your attention and extra focus going forward. Everything else on the original list — items that didn't survive either filter — represents a cognitive tax you're paying without any corresponding return; these are the "open background apps" draining mental energy without producing results.

A complementary set of questions extends this audit before adding anything new to your plate: What's currently on your plate that isn't producing anything meaningful? What are you holding onto purely out of guilt rather than because it's actually working for you? What would actually happen if you simply stopped doing it? The suggested practice is to ask these "remove" questions before looking for something new to add — reflexively reaching for addition is the default impulse, but asking what can be removed first is framed as having a much larger potential impact on someone's life than almost any other single change.
